The Enemy of the Good

The French writer Voltaire once asserted that "The perfect is the enemy of the good". This is an interesting concept, though not necessarily a new one, even for the time. Chinese philosopher Confucious advised "better a diamond with flaw than a pebble without" and legendary playwright William Shakespeare wrote "striving to better, we oft mar what's well". But, what does it mean?

The idea instantly seems to be an oxymoron because we all regard perfect as being superior to merely good. But, good seems far more quantifiable. Good implies something that is functional, that gets the job done . . . whatever that job may be. Perfect is a bit more ephemeral. At what point is something perfect? A machine that consumes raw material and ejects widgets is good, if the the produced widgets have a market value greater than all the input material (and energy, labor, etc.). But, what would a "perfect" machine be? One that produces no waste? One that produces the widgets instantly? That requires no energy or labor inputs? If every factory has labor-less machines, whose wages are even buying the product?

These are thorny questions and reasonable people can often quickly shun the perfect and choose to define "good" as "good enough". There is even a concept in management theory called Analysis Paralysis, which describes the inability to chose an option because of ever greater attention paid to every minute detail of the selections available. The irony, of course, is that such rigor is necessitated by the importance of the decision, but the decision made too late (or not at all), results in even greater harm or loss than the "bad" choice would have.

And what, you may ask, has all this to do with web design? Well, when choosing an option for a web designer, it is important to scale cost and service appropriately for the endeavor. One extreme of this was addressed in the article Do It Yourself. Therein, I explained that web design was a skill well within the means of most people to acquire, if they choose, but that sometimes it is more productive to outsource that function. However, it is possible to go too far in the idea that web designers are worth whatever they are paid.

This is not to say that I deny the skills of a designer charging $80, $100, or even more for an hour's labor. The product they provide may absolutely be worth that, in terms of the technical skill required to produce it or the performance of the code. Nonetheless, it may still not be appropriate for your needs. Let's say you are a baker and need a more tangible asset, like a truck to deliver your bread. A 25' long step-van might be just the ticket. But, if you analyze the capacity, cost, and fuel efficiency of a tractor-trailer rig, it is indisputably superior. Or is it?

The issue here is unused capacity. If you can fill that 80' long tractor-trailer with your delicious baked goods on the regular, you will pay less for each loaf thus moved. However, if you can't, then there are a lot of costs that will remain the same, even if the trailer is only half-full of product. Not the least of these is the cost of the truck itself and the wage of the driver. Needless to say, buying and operating a 25' step-van takes less money and skill than an 80' tractor-trailer rig.

A similar dynamic is at work with websites. Though your particular needs may require a professional designer, they may not necessarily indicate the services of a premier design firm, with all the attendant costs. I can assure you that you can find design firms that will make you a site twice as good as I can and it may only cost you ten times a much! Let's talk.
